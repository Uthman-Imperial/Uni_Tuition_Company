A-levels

What you need to study

Design Engineering at Imperial requires Maths at A* and is competitive — the subject choices you make now can genuinely set you apart.

Required
A*

Mathematics

You need A* in Maths. This is non-negotiable — it's the only subject-specific grade requirement.

Recommended subjects

Further Mathematics

Strongly recommended. Makes Year 1 Engineering Maths significantly more manageable and signals mathematical maturity to admissions.

Physics

Highly useful. Solid Mechanics, Electronics, and Thermofluids all draw heavily on A-level Physics foundations.

Chemistry

Helpful for Materials and Manufacturing. Not essential but gives you a head start understanding material properties.

Design Technology

Relevant to the creative and prototyping side of the course. Shows admissions you already think like a designer.

Personal statement

Telling your story

Your chance to show you think like a design engineer — not just that you've done the right subjects.

What admissions looks for

Evidence of independent thinking, genuine curiosity about design and engineering, and self-directed projects or experiments.

Structure tips

Lead with a hook, not a cliché. Show your intellectual journey. End with where you want to go and why DE specifically.

Projects and evidence

Any project — however small — that shows you build without being told to is gold. Describe what you made, what went wrong, and what you learned.

Common mistakes

Listing subjects you enjoy without showing why. Being vague about what design engineering means to you. Describing teamwork without saying what your role was.

ESAT prep

The admissions test

Computer-based, multiple choice. For Design Engineering you sit Maths 1 and Maths 2 — 27 questions each, 40 minutes each.

Maths 1

A-level core Maths territory — algebra, calculus, trigonometry, statistics, mechanics. 27 questions, 40 minutes.

Maths 2

Further Maths territory — complex numbers, matrices, further calculus, differential equations. 27 questions, 40 minutes.

When to sit it

Window 1: Oct 12–16, 2026. Window 2: Jan 4–8, 2027. Book through UAT-UK from 20 July 2026.

How to prepare

Past papers are your best resource. The style is fast and unforgiving — time management matters as much as content knowledge.

Portfolio prep

Two minutes to show everything

Choose carefully — quality over quantity. They want to see what you make when nobody asks you to.

What to include

Anything you've made, built, designed, or coded. Physical projects, digital tools, art — anything that shows you create without being told to.

What not to include

School coursework unless genuinely impressive. Generic content that could belong to anyone. Anything you can't talk about in depth.

How to present it

Lead with what it is and why you made it, then what you learned. Don't narrate every detail — let the work speak.

What they're really assessing

Whether you build independently. Whether you reflect on what you make. Whether you have genuine curiosity and initiative.

Common questions

What they actually ask

Open-ended and problem-based. They want to see how you think, not just what you know.

"Why Design Engineering?"

Be specific. Reference something real — a project, a problem, an experience. Vague answers about combining creativity and engineering don't stand out.

"Tell me about something you've built"

Have two or three examples ready. Talk about the problem, your approach, what failed, and what you'd do differently.

Technical maths and physics problems

Problems you haven't seen before. Think out loud — interviewers want to follow your reasoning process, not just your answer.

"What would you redesign and why?"

Pick something specific. Articulate the problem clearly before jumping to solutions. Show you observe the world critically.

Design task tips

Thinking on your feet

The goal isn't a perfect answer — it's showing how you approach an open problem under pressure.

Understand before you solve

Define the problem clearly before jumping to solutions. Interviewers want to see that you don't assume you already know what the problem is.

Think out loud

Your reasoning is more valuable than your conclusion. Narrate what constraints you're considering and what trade-offs you're making.

Don't panic at ambiguity

Vague briefs are intentional. Ask clarifying questions. Comfort with uncertainty is a core design engineering skill.

Sketch if you can

Even rough sketches help communicate ideas and show spatial thinking. Clarity matters far more than polish.

Entry requirements

What you need to get in

Imperial assesses applicants individually, but these are the hard benchmarks to hit.

A-levels: A*AA

The A* must be in Mathematics. The other two As can be in any subject, though Physics and Further Maths are strongly recommended.

International Baccalaureate: 39 points

With a 7 in Mathematics at Higher Level. Both Maths Analysis and Maths Applications syllabi are accepted.

ESAT required

You must sit Maths 1 and Maths 2. Your result is considered alongside the rest of your application — no minimum score is published.

Interview required

If shortlisted. Online, November to March. Not everyone who sits the ESAT gets an interview.
