Design Engineering at Imperial is a unique programme that sits at the intersection of engineering and design. Housed in the Dyson School, it trains engineers who can think creatively and designers who can build rigorously.

What makes it different?

Unlike pure engineering programmes, DE weaves human-centred design thinking throughout every year. You'll prototype, build, iterate and present in a studio environment inspired by the best design schools in the world.

Career destinations

Graduates go into product design, robotics, consulting, startups, and everything in between. The degree is genuinely respected across industries because it produces well-rounded thinkers who can make things.

Required subjects
Mathematics Physics

Both are essential. Imperial expect Maths and Physics as two of your three A-levels, with strong grades in both.

Typical offer

A*AA

The A* is expected in Maths or Physics. The third subject is open — Art, DT, Further Maths, Chemistry and CS are all common choices.

Recommended subjects
Design & Technology Art & Design Further Maths Computer Science

While not required, these show genuine interest in design and strengthen your portfolio and personal statement narrative.

EPQ & other qualifications

An EPQ on a design or engineering topic is looked upon favourably. IB applicants typically need 38–40 points with 6,6,6 at Higher Level including Maths and Physics.

Personal Statement

Writing your Personal Statement

Structure
Opening hook
Lead with a specific project, moment, or problem that sparked your interest in design engineering. Avoid generic opening lines.
Content
Show both sides
DE sits between engineering and design — your PS should reflect both. Show you can think analytically AND creatively.
Projects
Talk about making things
Built something, designed something, fixed something? Describe your process: problem → iteration → outcome. This is what DE is about.
ESAT Preparation

Engineering & Sciences Admissions Test

Format
Structure
Two 40-minute sections. Part 1 (Mathematics) is compulsory. Part 2 depends on your course — DE candidates take Physics.
Content
What's tested
A-level Maths and Physics at depth. Expect problem-solving under time pressure rather than rote recall.
Tips
How to prepare
Do past papers under timed conditions. Speed matters. Flag and skip, don't get stuck. Review mistakes systematically.
Interview & Portfolio

Interview Preparation

Format
What to expect
Around 20–30 minutes with one or two academics. You'll present your portfolio and discuss a design problem. It's conversational, not an interrogation.
Portfolio
Portfolio prep
Include 3–5 projects that show your design process. Sketches, prototypes, failures and iterations matter more than a polished final product.
Questions
Common questions
"Walk me through a project." "What would you change?" "Design a solution to X." Practise talking through your thinking out loud.

Life in the Dyson School is genuinely unlike any other department at Imperial. The studio culture means you spend a huge amount of time building, prototyping, and collaborating rather than sitting in lectures.

The studio

You'll have access to one of the best-equipped design studios in the UK — laser cutters, 3D printers, electronics benches, woodworking tools, and more. Many students spend late nights there because they genuinely want to, not because they have to.

Cohort size

DE takes around 70 students per year. It's small enough that you know everyone, and large enough for a real community. The cohort is close-knit by the nature of working together on projects constantly.

Workload

Expect it to be demanding. DE is project-heavy which means work doesn't always come with a clear end point. Time management and knowing when something is "good enough" are skills you'll develop fast.

About

Why we built this

de/guide is built by Imperial students, for the students who come next.

When I was applying to Imperial, I noticed something frustrating: for some of the most interesting, most niche courses at one of the world's best universities, there was almost no useful information anywhere. Official pages. Generic advice. Nothing that felt real.

So we built it ourselves.

de/guide is the resource I wished existed when I was in sixth form. It's written by people who've been through the application process, sat the ESAT, done the interview, and spent time inside these courses — not by admissions offices trying to sound approachable.

Who we are

I'm Uthman, a first-year Design Engineering student at Imperial College London. I'm working with other students across different courses to build this out — because the information asymmetry between applicants and students is real, and unnecessary.

What we cover

Right now: Design Engineering, Materials Science, Computer Science, and Geology. We're expanding. If you study at Imperial and want to contribute a guide for your course, get in touch.
