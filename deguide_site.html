A-levels

What you need to study

Design Engineering at Imperial requires Maths at A* and is competitive — but the subject choices you make now can genuinely set you apart.

Required
A*

Mathematics

You need A* in Maths. This is non-negotiable — it's the only subject-specific grade requirement.

Recommended subjects

Further Mathematics

Strongly recommended. It makes Year 1 Engineering Maths significantly more manageable and shows genuine mathematical maturity to admissions.

Physics

Highly useful. Solid Mechanics, Electronics, and Thermofluids all draw heavily on A-level Physics foundations.

Chemistry

Helpful for the Materials and Manufacturing module. Not essential but gives you a head start in understanding material properties.

Design Technology

Relevant to the creative and prototyping side of the course. Shows admissions you already think like a designer.

Personal statement

Telling your story

The personal statement is your chance to show you think like a design engineer — not just that you've done the right subjects.

What admissions looks for

Evidence of independent thinking, genuine curiosity about design and engineering, and self-directed projects.

Structure tips

Lead with a hook, not a cliché. Show your intellectual journey. End with where you want to go.

Projects and evidence

Any project — however small — that shows you build without being told to is gold. Describe what you made, what went wrong, and what you learned.

Common mistakes

Listing subjects you enjoy without showing why. Writing about teamwork without showing your role. Being vague about what design engineering actually means to you.

ESAT prep

The admissions test

The Engineering and Science Admissions Test (ESAT) is computer-based and multiple choice. For Design Engineering you sit Maths 1 and Maths 2.

Maths 1

27 questions, 40 minutes. Covers A-level core Maths — algebra, calculus, trigonometry, statistics.

Maths 2

27 questions, 40 minutes. Further Maths territory — complex numbers, matrices, further calculus.

When to sit it

Two windows: Oct 12–16, 2026 and Jan 4–8, 2027. Book through UAT-UK from 20 July 2026.

How to prepare

Past papers are your best resource. The style is fast and unforgiving — time management matters as much as content knowledge.

Portfolio prep

Two minutes to show everything

You're given up to 2 minutes at the end of the interview to show your own work or projects. Choose carefully — quality over quantity.

What to include

Anything you've made, built, designed, or coded. Physical projects, digital tools, art — anything that shows you create without being told to.

What not to include

School coursework unless it's genuinely impressive. Generic content that could belong to anyone. Anything you can't talk about in detail.

How to present it

Have it ready to share on screen. Lead with what it is and why you made it, then what you learned. Don't narrate every detail — let the work speak.

What they're really assessing

Whether you build things independently. Whether you reflect on what you make. Whether you have genuine curiosity and initiative.

Common questions

What they actually ask

Interview questions tend to be open-ended and problem-based. They want to see how you think, not just what you know.

"Why Design Engineering?"

Be specific. Reference something real — a project, a problem, an experience. Vague answers about "combining creativity and engineering" don't stand out.

"Tell me about something you've built"

This is where your portfolio moments come in. Have two or three ready. Talk about the problem, your approach, what failed, and what you'd do differently.

Technical maths and physics problems

Expect problems you haven't seen before. They're testing your reasoning process, not your memory. Think out loud — interviewers want to follow your logic.

"What would you redesign and why?"

A classic design thinking question. Show that you observe the world critically — pick something specific and articulate the problem clearly before jumping to solutions.

Design task tips

Thinking on your feet

You may be given a live design problem to work through. The goal isn't a perfect answer — it's showing how you approach an open problem.

Understand before you solve

Take time to define the problem clearly before jumping to solutions. Interviewers want to see that you don't assume you know what the problem is.

Think out loud

Your reasoning is more valuable than your conclusion. Narrate your thinking — what constraints you're considering, what trade-offs you're making.

Don't panic at ambiguity

Vague briefs are intentional. Ask clarifying questions. Show you're comfortable with uncertainty — that's a core design engineering skill.

Sketch if you can

Even rough sketches help communicate ideas and show spatial thinking. You don't need to be a brilliant illustrator — clarity matters more than polish.

Entry requirements

What you need to get in

Imperial assesses applicants individually, but these are the benchmarks to aim for.

A-levels: A*AA

The A* must be in Mathematics. The other two As can be in any subject, though Physics and Further Maths are strongly recommended.

International Baccalaureate: 39 points

With a 7 in Mathematics at Higher Level. Both Maths Analysis and Maths Applications syllabi are accepted.

ESAT required

You must sit Maths 1 and Maths 2. There is no minimum score published — your result is considered alongside the rest of your application.

Interview required

If shortlisted. Interviews run online from November to March. Not everyone who sits the ESAT gets an interview.
